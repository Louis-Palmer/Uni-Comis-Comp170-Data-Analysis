Finding ideal countries to adopt hospital bed sensors

Abstract:

This Study aims to answer Attensys.io's question: "Which countries would be most successful in the healthcare industry for Attensys.io's bed sensors?" The study uses data on bed counts per 1000 people to determine the best places for the sensors. The results showed further research is required, however it is narrowed to a few countries being Germany, Belarus, South Korea, North Korea, and Japan. These results differ slightly from my hypothesis.

Introduction:

Attensys.io are a company that are developing sensors in train seats, they considering expanding into the healthcare industry with sensors in hospital beds. The bed sensors can sense weight and moisture. I have been tasked with identifying which countries would be most successful for Attensys.io. I will collect data on the number of hospital beds for each country, focusing on the main two types of hospital beds; curative care (acute) and intensive care (ICU). Using this data, I will identify the places with the highest number of beds per 1000 people.

Literature Review

Brief Introduction

Sensors will be a key part in the healthcare industry for example, Attensys.io sensors can track data on length of bed stay and bed capacity. ”The ability to predict hospital bed capacity for different types of wards is essential for monitoring and planning purposes during epidemics”-[1]. They are also predicted to grow in popularity "The sensor market will exceed trillion sensors per year soon."-[2].

Healthcare systems rating

I could not find direct information about the use of sensors in hospital beds. Instead, I looked at a healthcare system ranking provided by Numbeo [3], seen in Figure 1. This is a worldwide ranking of countries based on surveys that “consider factors that contribute to overall health”- [4]. The top 5 countries on this ranking, from the 1st to the 5th are as follows: Taiwan, South Korea, Japan, France, and Denmark.

Per Capita Expenditure

I also looked at per capita healthcare expenditure. The top 5 countries according to Statista [5] from the 1st to the 5th are as follows: United States, Germany, Switzerland, Norway, and Netherlands.

Comparison

Comparing the healthcare system ratings and the expenditure, none of the mentioned countries appear to be ranked within the top 5 in either of the lists. Going further down the list, the first to appear on both lists is Denmark, which ranks 5th in healthcare rating and 7th in healthcare expenditure. Upon comparing these stats, we can see that the European Union tends to have higher expenditure for healthcare (compared to the average), apart from the United States. Asia also tends to have higher healthcare ratings compared to the average.

Healthcare Innovation

Another statistic worth considering is healthcare innovation. According to, The FREOPP World Index of Healthcare Innovation-[6], Switzerland is ranked 1st for healthcare innovation, followed by the Netherlands, Germany, Ireland, and Israel. It is likely that these countries are more open to new medical technology.

Hypothesis

My hypothesis is that Denmark, the Netherlands, and the United States will be the most likely countries where Attensys.io will find success. I choose these because they have high ratings in all 3 of the above categories. Other potential clients are Switzerland and Germany, which both score exceptionally high in expenditure. In Asia, Taiwan, South Korea, and Japan all have some of the highest rankings in healthcare rating. I could see them adopting the sensors after successful implementation in other countries.

Methodology

Data Source

My main source of data was through Kaggle, I obtained a dataset named 'Global Hospital Beds Capacity (for COVID-19)' [7] This contained multiple datasets, with hospital bed capacities for all countries. I was not able to find any other datasets related to this specific category. Therefore, I decided that obtaining the quantity of the hospital beds and types, would be the most beneficial.

Acute beds Europe

This bar chart, Figure 4, shows the number of acute hospital beds per 1000 people, for each country in the EU in 2017. Bulgaria is rated first with 6.1 acute beds per 1000 people, followed by Germany with 6 acute beds. Lithuania is third in line with 5.5 acute beds. I decided a bar chart would be the best representation for this data, as the entries are for EU countries only and not globally. The range of the stats were not spread out either.

ICU beds Germany

This chart shows ICU beds per 1000 for each state in Germany. This shows ICU beds are relatively scarce compared to acute beds. Despite Germany being second in Europe for acute beds, Germany only has an average of 0.43 ICU beds, which is nearly 12 times less than its acute beds. Even in Berlin, Germany, there are only 0.76 ICU beds, which is almost 8 times less than its acute beds. This gives us more insight into which geographical locations are best to target in the chosen countries. The capitals being the most likely places, as they tend to have higher bed counts compared to the rest of the country. For this report we will be focusing on countries, not specific states, or cities within them.

Heatmaps

When I generated the Data for each country as a bar chart, the chart was incredibly long and nearly impossible to read, as it was so crammed. I found a heatmap is the best way to present data for each country.

Figure 6, is a greyscale heatmap showing the total of beds across multiple countries, using data from multiple years, prioritizing the most recent years. Visualizing data as a heatmap allows us to recognize patterns and areas, instead of just looking at countries individually. The data shows the EU has the highest average bed count compared to other places, with a few exceptions like South Korea, North Korea, Russia, and Japan.

Heatmap Issues

A few issues with this heatmap are for example, data for Namibia and South Sudan are null, being displayed as being blank/white. To make this more noticeable I changed the colour scheme.

Results:

Main Result

The data identified that Europe and Asia have the highest bed count. Europe had a consistent average, across most of the countries. Asia has few countries that are noticeable, being South Korea, North Korea, and Japan. In Europe, Belarus, Ukraine, Germany, and Russia (putting it in Europe due to similarities) are countries that stand out the most. This disproves my hypothesis where I thought Denmark, Netherlands, and the United States would be the main countries, but none of these countries stand out on my map. Even Denmark and the Netherlands, compared to the rest of Europe, don’t stand out, they have some of the fewest beds per 1000 people. The Unites States it was not very noticeable at all even with its physical size on the map which would influence noticeability. The only country that I mentioned in my hypothesis was Germany that stood out in Europe.

Secondary Results

In Asia, the countries I discussed in my hypothesis were South Korea, Japan, and Taiwan. Comparing it to Figure 6, South Korea and Japan with North Korea stood out extremely well having some of the highest bed counts and higher land mass. Taiwan was not as visible. So therefore, part of my hypothesis is inaccurate.

Unreliability

It is worth noting that the Figure 6 results are across the span of multiple years, ranging from 1970-2019. For example, North Korea's data was recorded in 1970; it is worth considering ignoring North Korea, as a lot could have changed since then. It is important to keep this in mind and acknowledge further research is required.

Discussion:

Patterns

In Europe, a pattern was discovered, having a consistency and a higher-than-average-score for bed counts. While Asia’s average score for bed counts was lower overall, it had some of the highest for bed count countries being South Korea, North Korea, and Japan.

Unreliability

It is also worth noting that my analysis assumes that countries with higher bed counts are ideal places to target. This will most likely not be 100% reliable as sensors could have multiple different uses, being more effective elsewhere. Another argument is that places with lower bed counts would be better places to start for testing, this could also lead to gaining successful implementation mentioned in the hypothesis. Further research would be required.

Conclusion:

Key Points

In Asia the countries, South Korea, North Korea, and Japan have the highest bed counts. In Europe most countries had an above average bed count, whilst also being more consistent with no anomalies.

Comparing the Hypothesis

The results contrast with my hypothesis, which does not contain any of the countries that were major contenders in terms of bed count; apart from, South Korea, Japan, and Germany which appear in both the hypothesis and the results.

Recommendation

Main

For Attensys.io, I recommend further research to decide which countries to prioritize. However, based on my results, I would recommend narrowing down the research to 5 countries: Germany, Belarus, South Korea, North Korea, and Japan.

Reasons

Germany appears to be at the centre of the highly rated countries, in terms of healthcare innovation and healthcare systems, itself also being highly rated in both. In Europe, Belarus has the second highest in terms of bed count after Monaco. Overall, in terms of bed counts, South Korea and Japan were the highest globally and were also in the hypothesis. Despite geopolitical situation, North Korea also has a high bed count.
